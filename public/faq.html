Rentable FAQ

Purchase Price

Definition: The amount the buyer pays for the property.
How it Applies: The purchase price is combined with all closing costs and any renovation costs to make up the initial investment. These are up front, one time costs, as opposed to recurring costs like taxes, utilities, repairs, etc. The initial investment is compared to the net rent to determine the yield.
Tips: Adjust the purchase price up and down while keeping all other variables the same to get a clear picture of how the purchase price is affecting your yield.

Projected Rent

Definition:The total monthly payment collected for use of the property.
How it Applies:Your projected monthly rent input will automatically be multiplied by twelve to determine the yearly rent. The recurring costs are then subtracted from the yearly rent. Those costs include property taxes, utilities, repair and maintenance, insurance, and any other recurring costs. The yearly rental rate minus all of those recurring costs equals the net yearly rental rate. That number is then divided by the total.

Yearly Rent = (Monthly Rent * 12)

Yearly Rent - (recurring costs) = Net Yearly Rent

Net Yearly Rent/Initial Investment = Yield

Tips: Adjust the projected rent up and down while keeping all other variables the same to get a clear picture of how the projected rent is affecting your yield. Be sure to include all income the property produces, including, e.g., laundry income or vending machine income.

Yearly Taxes

Definition:The total yearly property taxes owed.
How it Applies:Yearly Taxes are subtracted from Yearly Rent (along with other recurring costs) to determine net yearly rent.

Yearly Rent = (Monthly Rent * 12)

Yearly Rent - (recurring costs) = Net Yearly Rent

Net Yearly Rent/Initial Investment = Yield

Tips: The property taxes are typically available on local municipal websites. Not all tax rates are the same, so determining the local rate on a potential investment is important. Depending on the region, tax abatements (or other tax reduction programs) may be available, which can affect the yearly Tax. If you can't find the Yearly Tax anywhere, use 1% of the total purchase price as an estimate.

Yearly Maintenance Cost

Definition:The total amount spent per year on costs towards upkeep of the property.
How it Applies:Yearly Maintenance Costs are subtracted from Yearly Rent (along with other recurring costs) to determine Net Yearly Rent.

Yearly Rent = (Monthly Rent * 12)

Yearly Rent - (recurring costs) = Net Yearly Rent

Net Yearly Rent/Initial Investment = Yield

Tips: Yearly Maintenance can be tough to predict. Typically newer properties require less maintenance due to newer systems (such as HVAC). If you aren't sure of the potential cost, use 1% of the total projected purchase price.

Renovation Costs

Definition:The upfront costs used towards the improvement of the Property.
How it Applies:Renovation Costs are a one time, upfront cost. They are added to the purchase price (along with any closing costs) to determine the initial investment. As the renovation costs only happen once, they do not affect the Net Yearly Rent.

Initial Investment = (Purchase Price + Renovation Costs + [any other initial costs])

Yearly Rent - (recurring costs) = Net Yearly Rent

Net Yearly Rent/Initial Investment = Yield

Tips: Not all investments require renovation costs. If the property is already in good condition, the renovation costs may be zero. Also, often buyers will receive a credit towards maintenance repairs as part of the agreement of sale (depending on the nature of the transaction). If you aren't sure of the potential renovation costs, leave it as zero.

Homeowners Insurance

Definition:The yearly cost applied to insuring the Property.
How it Applies:Insurance is a yearly recurring cost, so it is subtracted from the Yearly Rent (along with any other recurring costs) to determine Net Yearly Rent.

Initial Investment = (Purchase Price + Renovation Costs + [any other initial costs])

Yearly Rent - (recurring costs) = Net Yearly Rent

Net Yearly Rent/Initial Investment = Yield

Tips: While insurance isn't technically required, most investments are insured (and if you intend to use a mortgage to purchase the property, you will be required to have insurance). If you aren't sure of the potential insurance costs, use .0025% of the property. For example, if the Projected Purchase price is $400,000, expect insurance to cost somewhere around $1,250. However, many factors can affect your actual rate, such as location, residential/commercial, nature of the investment, etc.

Utilities

Definition:The monthly costs paid by the owner of the property, rather than the tenant.
How it Applies:Utilities are a recurring cost subtracted from the Yearly Rent (along with other recurring costs) to determine the Net Yearly Rent. Your monthly utility input will automatically be multiplied by twelve to determine the yearly utility costs.

Yearly Utility Costs = (Monthly Utility Costs * 12)

Initial Investment = (Purchase Price + Renovation Costs + [any other initial costs])

Yearly Rent - (recurring costs) = Net Yearly Rent

Net Yearly Rent/Initial Investment = Yield

Tips:Utilities are often paid by the tenant, but occasionally the investor will pay some or all of the utilities. If you aren't sure of the potential utility costs, leave them at zero.

Vacancy Rate

Definition:The percentage of time that the Property is not occupied by a tenant and is not producing rent.
How it Applies:The Vacancy Rate percentage is multiplied by the total rental revenue. That number is then subtracted from the total rental revenue. Think of it as the Vacancy Rate accounting for the months that you aren't collecting any rent. Total Rental Revenue = (Monthly Total Rent x 12) Yearly Rent = Total Rental Revenue - (Total Rental Revenue * Vacancy Rate) For Example: $95 Yearly Rent = $100 Total Rental Revenue - ($100 Total Rental Revenue * 5% Total Rental Revenue)

Total Rental Revenue = (Monthly Total Rent x 12)

Yearly Rent = Total Rental Revenue - (Total Rental Revenue * Vacancy Rate)

For Example:

$95 Yearly Rent = $100 Total Rental Revenue - ($100 Total Rental Revenue * 5% Total Rental Revenue)

Tips:Vacancy rate can be tough to predict and often depends on the nature of the property. For some context, one momnth of vacancy per year is a vacancy rate of roughly 8.33%. If you aren't sure of the potential vacancy rate, use 8.33%.

Closing Costs

Definition:The extra costs associated with purchasing the Property, such as transfer tax, mortgage fees, title fees,etc. They are essentially all the one time costs that aren't accounted for the Purchase Price and the Renovation Costs.
How it Applies:Closing costs are a one time cost, so they apply to the initial investment (as opposed to reoccuring costs, which apply to the Yearly Rent). The closing costs are combined with the Purchase Price and the Renovation Cost, collectively making up the Initial Investment. Initial Investment = (Purchase Price + Renovation Costs + Closing Costs) Closing Costs typically consist primarily of: Transfer Taxes (which depend on the local rate) Any mortgage fees such as origination costs Title Insurance/Fees

Initial Investment = (Purchase Price + Renovation Costs + Closing Costs)


Closing Costs typically consist primarily of:

Transfer Taxes (which depend on the local rate)

Any mortgage fees such as origination costs

Title Insurance/Fees

Tips:Closing costs depend on the nature of the transaction. If the purchase is unleveraged (meaning there is no mortgage involved), the closing costs should be significantly reduced. Also,the transfer tax (which is completely separate from the yearly property tax) is likely to be the largest of the closing costs, so determining the local transfer tax rate is a good place to start when determining the projected closing costs. If you aren't sure of the potential closing costs, use 3% of the total purchase price.
